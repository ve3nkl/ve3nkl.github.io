Wearable Sattelite Station

This satellite station was built to work SSB satellites. From the very beginning I wanted to have a “wearable” station, the one where you would not require any surfaces to put the radios on or a tripod to mount the antenna. The frequency would have to be adjusted manually - no computers.

As the two radios I have are Yaesu FT-817ND and Elecraft KX3 with the 2m option installed, my initial thought was to put both radios in a single bag that could be worn using a shoulder strap. The Arrow antenna could be hand held, of cause. Unfortunately, this simple design did not work for me very well. Having the two radios really close together caused desense in the receiver when the other radio was transmitting.

This issue was not difficult to resolve: the Elecraft radio remained in the bag but the Yaesu was moved on my other shoulder. In fact, now I could use a smaller bag for the Elecraft rig.

On the picture above you can see both radios. Yaesu FT-817ND has a spacer made of PVC fittings attached to it. Its purpose is to provide some space between the controls and the body so that the controls are not altered accidentally. You can also see a morse code Pico Paddle from Palm Radio attached to the Yeasu rig. Although it is not visible very well on the picture but there is a voice recorder located between the Elecraft rig and the body.

This recorder has a very useful feature. You can monitor your audio being recorded. The input of the recorder is connected to the phone output of the receive radio and the output goes to the head phones. The recorder won’t provide any output unless it is in the recording mode. If you can hear the signal in your phones it means it is being recorded. This way I cannot simply forget to press the “record” button…

When I tried to use this setup I found another problem: I have only two hands. Not that I never noticed this before, but I have never felt such a desperate need for the third one. It would be really great to be able to fine-turn your frequency during the transmit. But pressing the PTT button, turning the frequency nob and holding the antenna with only two hands is a great challenge.

The solution came in the form of an external PTT button mounted on the Arrow boom, right next to the handle.

It was made of a PVC tube cap and a metal angle piece left from assembling a furniture (why there are always extra parts left ?). I shaped it to serve as a brace. A glue gun was used to keep in place a micro button inserted in the opening of the PVC cap.

When I work a Satellite having its uplink on UHF and its downlink on VHF, I have to connect the mic and the PTT button to the Yaesu rig and the phones to the Elecraft rig (in fact, there is also a voice recorder in between). Now consider this: I am using a Heil Sound headset with a two-pin connector for an ICOM handheld radios, a Yeasu rig, an Elecraft rig and a homebrew PTT button. To connect all my zoo together I had to use a small interface box (another homebrew project) shown in the picture below.

The blue plug (made of a piece of an old Ethernet cable) is connected to the Yaesu RJ-45 mic socket. The light green audio plug is for the phones socket (in my case I connect it to the output of the voice recorder). The single socket on the left picture is for a cable coming from my PTT button and the two sockets on the right picture are compatible with the double-pin ICOM audio standard (for my Heil Sound headset). Neddless to say that when I tried my design for the first time there was no incoming audio from the mic. Spending an hour with the Yaesu schematics helped me to come up with an idea of inserting an additional resistor to match the input Yaesu circuit properly. It worked ! One tiny detail left to be told. The Heil Sound headset I am using has its own PTT button on its cable. Even though my PTT puts Yaesu in the TX mode, the audio from the headset mic won’t come in unless the headset’s PTT button is also depressed. So in order to work properly this headset’s PTT button have to be kept in the pushed down position. As usual, PVC tubes come to the rescue…

A cut in the piece of tube is done to create a spring action. A small piece of soft plastic (it was part of a mirror mounting kit) is used to press on the PTT button.
